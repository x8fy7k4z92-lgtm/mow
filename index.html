Business
Owners

For business owners, the company is not just an asset — it is often the largest, most concentrated, and most emotionally significant piece of the financial picture. That requires a different kind of planning conversation.

Whether you are years away from an exit or actively in the process, the decisions made now have lasting consequences: how the sale is structured, how proceeds are invested, how much goes to taxes versus your family. Most owners spend years building the business and relatively little time planning for what comes after. We help close that gap.

Our work with business owners typically spans three distinct phases. Pre-exit: identifying the right structure, coordinating with M&A advisors and attorneys, modeling after-tax outcomes, and making sure your personal financial plan can absorb the transition. At exit: managing the liquidity event itself, coordinating the tax strategy, and establishing the investment framework for the proceeds. Post-exit: rebuilding a portfolio that reflects your new financial reality — without the concentrated risk of the business, but with a structure that preserves what you built.

Senior
Executives

Senior executives face a wealth management challenge that is fundamentally different from what most advisors are built to handle: income and assets that arrive in multiple forms, on irregular schedules, with significant tax consequences attached to each decision.

Base salary is the straightforward part. The complexity lies in everything else — RSUs vesting across multiple tranches, NQSOs and ISOs with overlapping exercise windows, deferred compensation plans with payout elections that have already been locked in, and concentrated positions in employer stock that represent both the foundation of your wealth and its biggest single risk. Each of these requires a coordinated strategy. Handled in isolation, they add up to unnecessary tax liability and poorly diversified exposure.

The transition from executive income to investment income — retirement or a career change — is where the planning stakes are highest. The decisions made in the 24–36 months on either side of that transition typically have more impact on long-term financial outcomes than any other period. We work with executives to build a plan that accounts for what is coming, not just what is here today.

Planning areas we address

  • · Equity compensation strategy — RSU vesting schedules, ISO/NQSO exercise planning, and AMT exposure
  • · Concentrated stock position management: staged diversification, exchange funds, and covered call strategies
  • · Deferred compensation plan coordination — payout election review and distribution timing
  • · 10b5-1 plan strategy for orderly stock sales within trading windows
  • · Retirement transition modeling — income replacement, Social Security optimization, Medicare planning
  • · Executive benefit review: SERP, COLI, supplemental life, and non-qualified plans
  • · Asset location across taxable, tax-deferred, and Roth accounts
  • · Estate planning for accumulated wealth: trusts, gifting strategies, beneficiary coordination

Who We Serve

Families in
Transition

Some of the most consequential financial decisions happen not during periods of stability, but during transition — retirement, inheritance, divorce, the death of a spouse, or a significant change in household income. These moments require clear thinking precisely when clarity is hardest to find.

Approaching retirement is the most common inflection point we see. The accumulation phase — saving, investing, deferring — is governed by a relatively simple framework. The distribution phase is far more complex: sequencing withdrawals across account types, managing taxes on Social Security and RMDs, deciding when to take pension elections, and structuring a sustainable income stream that does not outlive the people it supports. Getting this right in the first few years of retirement matters more than most people expect.

Inheritance and estate settlement present their own set of challenges. Suddenly inheriting significant assets — particularly if they arrive as a mix of IRAs, taxable accounts, real estate, and business interests — requires immediate decisions with long-term implications. We help families navigate these moments without rushing into decisions that cannot easily be reversed.

Divorce, widowhood, and other major life changes often leave one spouse managing the finances for the first time. We approach these conversations with patience and care — providing clarity without pressure, and a plan that reflects the new reality rather than the old one.

Planning areas we address

  • · Retirement income planning — withdrawal sequencing, RMD strategy, and sustainable distribution rates
  • · Social Security optimization and pension election analysis
  • · Inherited IRA and beneficiary account strategy under current SECURE Act rules
  • · Estate settlement coordination — navigating the financial complexity of administering an estate
  • · Divorce financial planning — asset division analysis, QDRO review, and post-settlement restructuring
  • · Long-term care planning and healthcare cost modeling
  • · Portfolio restructuring to reflect a new income profile and risk tolerance
  • · Beneficiary review and updated estate documents following a life change

Who We Serve

New Hampshire
Relocators

New Hampshire is one of the most tax-advantaged states in the country. No income tax on wages, no sales tax, and no estate tax. For individuals moving from Massachusetts, Connecticut, New York, or California, the financial implications are significant — but only if the transition is planned correctly.

Establishing domicile in New Hampshire is not simply a matter of updating your driver's license and voter registration. States with income taxes — particularly Massachusetts, which aggressively audits departing high-income residents — scrutinize relocations carefully. The burden of proof is on the taxpayer to demonstrate that New Hampshire is the true primary residence. That means documentation, behavioral changes, and in some cases, a coordinated communication strategy with a tax attorney. We help new clients understand what is required and connect them with the right professionals when the stakes are high.

Beyond the tax residency question, relocating to New Hampshire typically means rebuilding an advisory team from scratch. Your prior financial advisor, CPA, estate attorney, and insurance relationships were likely anchored to where you used to live. We help new residents establish a coordinated local advisory structure — and serve as the financial planning hub around which other relationships are organized.

New Hampshire also has no estate tax, which changes the calculus on certain estate planning structures that may have been established in a higher-tax state. Trusts, gifting programs, and charitable vehicles that made sense in Massachusetts or New York may need to be revisited in light of your new domicile.

Planning areas we address

  • · Domicile transition planning — what Massachusetts and other states require to recognize the move
  • · Coordination with tax counsel on residency audits and multi-state filing obligations
  • · New Hampshire tax landscape: no income tax, no sales tax, no estate tax — what that means for your plan
  • · Investment income and interest/dividend considerations under NH's prior tax structure
  • · Estate plan review — updating documents to reflect NH domicile and revised exposure
  • · Advisory team rebuild: introductions to local CPAs, estate attorneys, insurance advisors
  • · Real estate and mortgage coordination for the primary residence transition
  • · Retirement income restructuring to take full advantage of the NH tax profile

Well-suited for relocators who...

  • · Are moving from Massachusetts, Connecticut, New York, or another high-tax state with income above $500K
  • · Have not yet established a trusted financial advisory relationship in New Hampshire
  • · Want to understand what the NH tax advantage actually means for their specific situation — not just in general terms
  • · Are relocating in connection with a business exit, retirement, or other major financial transition

Service Overview

Tax-Aware
Investment Strategy

At $5–15M in assets, the difference between tax-aware and tax-indifferent investing can amount to hundreds of thousands of dollars over a decade. After-tax return is the only return that matters.

Most investment managers treat tax as an accounting problem — something to sort out at year-end with your CPA. We treat it as a design constraint from the start. Every portfolio decision is evaluated through its after-tax impact.

New Hampshire's lack of an income tax on wages and investment income creates a specific planning environment for our clients — one that requires different strategies than clients in Massachusetts, Connecticut, or New York often arrive with. We understand that transition.

Core Strategies We Deploy

  • ◆Asset location optimization — placing tax-inefficient assets (bonds, REITs, actively managed funds) in tax-deferred accounts and tax-efficient assets in taxable accounts to reduce your annual tax drag.
  • ◆Tax-loss harvesting — systematically realizing losses to offset gains elsewhere in your portfolio, reducing your current-year tax liability without materially changing your investment exposure.
  • ◆Roth conversion planning — identifying low-income years or retirement transition windows where converting traditional IRA assets to Roth at a lower rate creates long-term tax advantage.
  • ◆Capital gains management — timing the realization of long-term gains to minimize bracket exposure, particularly important for clients with significant unrealized appreciation.
  • ◆Qualified Opportunity Zone and charitable strategies — for clients with large capital gains events, we evaluate deferral and elimination strategies in coordination with your tax advisor.
  • ◆Business exit tax planning — for business owners approaching a liquidity event, pre-transaction planning can materially change the net proceeds. Early coordination is essential.

New Hampshire-Specific Considerations

New Hampshire does not tax earned income or capital gains, which creates meaningful planning opportunities for clients relocating from higher-tax states — but also requires revisiting strategies built under a different tax regime. We help clients make that transition thoughtfully.

We work in close coordination with your CPA and do not operate in a vacuum. Tax-aware investing requires communication across your advisory team, and we are experienced facilitating that collaboration.

This service is well-suited for clients who

  • · Are relocating to New Hampshire from a higher-tax state
  • · Have significant unrealized gains and no clear plan for managing them
  • · Are approaching or navigating a business exit or liquidity event
  • · Hold concentrated equity compensation (RSUs, stock options, NQSOs)
  • · Feel their current portfolio is not being managed with tax in mind

Service Overview

Legacy &
Estate Planning

An estate plan is not a document. It is a set of decisions about what you want your wealth to accomplish — for your family, your community, and the people who matter most to you.

At $5–15M, most families are well inside federal estate tax thresholds today — but those thresholds are scheduled to change materially in 2026, and state-level exposure varies. The planning window is now. Families who act thoughtfully in the next few years will have significantly more flexibility than those who wait.

We do not draft legal documents — that is the role of your estate attorney. Our role is to ensure the financial strategy and the legal structure are aligned, and that the conversation about legacy happens proactively rather than reactively.

Planning Areas We Coordinate

  • ◆Estate structure review — evaluating existing wills, trusts, and beneficiary designations to ensure they reflect your current wishes and are aligned with your financial plan.
  • ◆Gifting strategies — annual exclusion gifting, 529 superfunding, and coordinated transfers to reduce future estate exposure while benefiting family members during your lifetime.
  • ◆Trust planning coordination — working with your estate attorney on revocable and irrevocable trust structures, including SLATs, GRATs, and ILITs where appropriate.
  • ◆Charitable planning — Donor Advised Funds, Charitable Remainder Trusts, and qualified charitable distributions for clients with philanthropic goals who want to give efficiently.
  • ◆Beneficiary and titling review — ensuring that account ownership, beneficiary designations, and titling structures are consistent across your financial life and aligned with your estate documents.
  • ◆Multi-generational wealth conversations — for clients thinking about how to prepare the next generation, we facilitate family financial conversations and help establish a framework for wealth transfer that reflects your values.

A Note on the 2026 Estate Tax Sunset

The Tax Cuts and Jobs Act doubled the federal estate tax exemption in 2018. That doubling is currently set to expire at the end of 2025, reverting the exemption from approximately $13.6M per individual to roughly $7M. For married couples with combined estates in the $10–15M range, this is a meaningful planning issue — and the window to act is narrowing.

We are actively working with clients on this issue now. If your estate plan has not been reviewed in the past 12–18 months, it is worth a conversation.

This service is well-suited for clients who

  • · Have not reviewed their estate documents in the past 2–3 years
  • · Have a combined estate approaching or above $7M given the coming exemption change
  • · Want to transfer wealth to children or grandchildren in a tax-efficient way
  • · Have philanthropic goals but no clear vehicle or strategy
  • · Are business owners whose succession planning intersects with their estateplan

Relocators

New Hampshire is one of the most tax-advantaged states in the country. No income tax on wages, no sales tax, and no estate tax. For individuals moving from Massachusetts, Connecticut, New York, or California, the financial implications are significant — but only if the transition is planned correctly.

Establishing domicile in New Hampshire is not simply a matter of updating your driver's license and voter registration. States with income taxes — particularly Massachusetts, which aggressively audits departing high-income residents — scrutinize relocations carefully. The burden of proof is on the taxpayer to demonstrate that New Hampshire is the true primary residence. That means documentation, behavioral changes, and in some cases, a coordinated communication strategy with a tax attorney. We help new clients understand what is required and connect them with the right professionals when the stakes are high.

Beyond the tax residency question, relocating to New Hampshire typically means rebuilding an advisory team from scratch. Your prior financial advisor, CPA, estate attorney, and insurance relationships were likely anchored to where you used to live. We help new residents establish a coordinated local advisory structure — and serve as the financial planning hub around which other relationships are organized.

New Hampshire also has no estate tax, which changes the calculus on certain estate planning structures that may have been established in a higher-tax state. Trusts, gifting programs, and charitable vehicles that made sense in Massachusetts or New York may need to be revisited in light of your new domicile.
